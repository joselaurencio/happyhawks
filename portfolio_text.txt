Engineering Design Process

Intake development
The Intake system underwent multiple development iterations: Initially we struggles to achieve a balance of rotational speed and
torque, while the feeder experienced problems with inconsistent compression throughout as well as dead spots. The diverter also had
its own problems allowing the intake of 2 or 4 artifacts. The most significant limiting factor was the limited intake size of previous
designs.
Our intake iterated from a single 6000 rpm motor, to
a coupled 
1150 rpm design, and finally a single 1150 motor with
a 5:7 ratio motor to optimize speed to torque ratio.
The feeder required many minor optimizations
to the boot kickers in order to perfect the
artifacts compresion with the guided hoods.
The diverter was designed to meet several key requirements.
Its length was optimized to fit within the robot while still
creating a specified angle for the guided shooter, and its
width was optimized to eliminate detrimental dead zones
without slowing intake speed. A lofted gradient was also
added to direct forces up and toward an intended path.
Achieving this required many 3D iterations.
When optimizing for cycle times, the size of the intake are critical.
Our original REV-based design could only collect one artifact at a
time, making it highly inefficient. The first iteration of our goBILDA-
based design improved capacity to two artifacts but proved awkward
in practice. In the final iteration, we implemented an overhang
intake, significantly increasing efficiency by allowing the robot to
collect multiple artifacts at once. This design widened the margin of
error for drivers, resulting in faster and consistent cycle times.

Software: Motion Control & Pathing
PedroPathing is an advanced Reactive Vector Follower developed by FTC Team 10158. It uses Bézier
curve generation to produce smooth, fast, and precise autonomous trajectories. Unlike time-based
followers, PedroPathing is not speed-limited by its paths, allowing us to maximize our scoring and
iteration within the 30-second autonomous window.
Why PedroPathing over Roadrunner
Through testing, we found PedroPathing was more precise at
higher accelerations and better at correcting unexpected
disturbances mid-path, which directly improves autonomous
consistency.
The Math Behind Reactive Vector Followers
Pedro Pathing combines an attractive force pulling
the robot toward the goal with repulsive forces
avoiding obstacles. Recalculating the ideal
movement vector every loop cycle. Overall
pedropathing has so many good features its hard

4 Autonom ous Strategy
We developed four autonomous routines for
different starting positions on the field, allowing
our robot to adapt based on alliance strategy and
partner positioning. These routines are split
between starting near the GOAL and starting in the
back LAUNCH ZONE, each optimized for efficient
pathing, fast cycling, and consistent scoring. By
using smooth trajectories and coordinated
intake/shooting sequences, each autonomous has
the potential to score up to 12 ARTIFACTS while
minimizing downtime and maximizing reliability.
FSM AUTO FLOW
IDLE  →   SPINUP   →  LAUNCH
Our autonomous routine uses a 4-state Finite State
Machine. The robot begins IDLE, then skips using
the Limelight 3A to ALIGN to the target since the
robot can just go to a preset point, SPINs UP the
shooter to the calculated RPM, then LAUNCHes. This
structure ensures each action only begins when the
previous is complete, improving reliability.

PD Alignm ent Loop
The alignment system uses a PD (Proportional-
Derivative) controller driven by the Limelight's
horizontal angle (tx) as the error signal — tuned
to kP = 0.02, kD = 0.003, deadband = 0.9°, and
maxPower = 0.4. The robot continuously corrects
its heading until tx falls within the deadband, at
which point the FSM advances to SPINUP. Through
comparative testing against a manual alignment
approach assisted by RGB LED feedback, we found
that experienced drivers could align faster
manually. So for now the PD loop serves primarily
as a fallback and consistency tool for newer
drivers or high-pressure matches.
LED Alignm ent Indicator
A GoBilda PWM RGB LED gives drivers real-
time alignment feedback. It turns red when
the robot is outside the deadband and
switches to green the moment alignment is
achieved — eliminating guesswork during
teleop.
GoBilda Pinpoint + Encoders
The Pinpoint odometry computer
processes encoder ticks from two dead
wheels to provide accurate field-
relative positioning. Combined with the
Limelight's relocalization capability, the
robot maintains positional accuracy
even after collisions or disturbances.

Physics-Based Model - Research in Progress
Our programming lead began developing a physics-based shooter model as an
alternative to empirical tuning. The model aimed to calculate required RPM
from physics principles  using projectile motion, flywheel inertia, and game
piece aerodynamics rather than collected data points. While the approach was
theoretically more generalizable, it was not implemented this season due to
the complexity of tuning the physical constants accurately within our timeline.

Software: Architecture & Teleop
Finite State Machine Architecture
Our entire robot is controlled through a Finite State Machine.
Rather than running commands in sequence, the FSM defines
discrete robot states and the conditions required to transition
between them. This makes the code modular, easy to debug, and
reliable under match pressure.
Flow diagram (main teleop FSM):
IDLE → ALIGN → SPINUP → LAUNCH → (back to IDLE)
Each state has a clear entry condition, active behavior, and exit
condition. No state can be skipped, preventing partial execution
errors.
Why FSM over Linear Code
Linear OpMode FSM
Steps run in order, blockingStates run in parallel, non-
blocking
Hard to interrupt mid-action Clean transitions at any point
Difficult to debug Each state is isolated and testable
Teleop Highlights
Self-Aligning: Driver holds a button → FSM transitions to ALIGN → Limelight PD loop corrects heading automatically → releases control back to
driver once locked on
Auto-RPM: Once aligned, shooter PIDF spins up to the regression-calculated RPM automatically — driver only needs to trigger launch
Stall Protection: Intake motor monitors current draw; when a stall is detected, the motor cuts power automatically to prevent burnout (directly
addresses the burned motor issue from earlier in the season)
Dual Launch Modes (Vision & Manual): The robot supports two shooting modes toggled with the BACK button. In Vision Mode, the Limelight
calculates distance and automatically sets the flywheel RPM. In Manual Mode, the driver aims freely while the robot still pulls a distance-based
RPM snapshot from the Limelight at the moment of trigger — preserving accuracy without requiring alignment.
Dynamic Target Offset: Rather than using a fixed alignment target, the robot calculates a distance-dependent horizontal offset for each
shooter left or right (BASE_OFFSET + distance × DISTANCE_FACTOR ± SHOOTER_OFFSET), accounting for the physical separation between the two
flywheels at varying ranges.
RGB Alignment Indicator: A GoBilda PWM RGB LED linearly interpolates between red and green based on alignment error. It turns fully green
only when tx falls within the deadband — giving drivers a clear visual lock confirmation without reading telemetry.
Manual Shot Data Logging: During manual shots, the robot logs the exact velocity target via telemetry (Manual L/R target TPS). This allowed
the team to collect RPM-at-distance data points at known field positions, which directly fed into building and validating the shooter regression
model.
